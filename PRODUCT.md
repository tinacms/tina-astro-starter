# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Two audiences meet on the same surface:

- **Developers evaluating or adopting TinaCMS** with Astro. They reach this starter via `npm create tina-app`, the docs, or a "Deploy" button. They are judging, in the first ten seconds, whether Tina produces something credible and modern. Their context is a fresh clone on localhost or a one-click deploy preview.
- **Content editors** who will use Tina's visual editing on top of this template. They experience the site through the rendered pages and the in-context editing islands.

The job to be done: prove that "Astro + TinaCMS" yields a polished, real-feeling site out of the box, with zero design cleanup required before showing it to a stakeholder.

## Product Purpose

This is the flagship Astro + TinaCMS starter template. It exists to be the reference first impression of the stack: a content/blog site (home, blog index, blog post) wired for Tina's React-free visual editing. Success is a fresh install that looks intentional and shippable on first load, on mobile and desktop, with no obvious placeholder or unstyled sections. The template should make a developer think "I could launch this today" and make Tina look like a tool that respects design.

## Brand Personality

Developer-modern and clean, a neutral canvas that lets the content carry the character. Three words: **credible, crafted, cosmic**.

The starter wears a light, Astro-fitting space theme: a warm Tina-ember accent and restrained celestial motifs, used to add personality without crowding the content.

- Crisp and trustworthy like the tools developers already respect (Linear, Vercel, Stripe), but content-first rather than dashboard-shaped.
- Tina ember orange, applied with restraint: a single ember accent (links, one CTA, focus states, small celestial motifs) on indigo-tinted neutrals, with a deep-space dark mode. Color stays rationed to roughly a tenth of any screen; it never floods surfaces or competes with the content.
- Content-forward: the writing, blocks, and images are the hero; the interface recedes so the page carries itself.

The tone is the expert who doesn't need to shout. Polished, never decorative for its own sake.

## Anti-references

- **Generic AI / SaaS template.** No hero-metric blocks (big number + small label + gradient), no endless identical icon-card grids, no **flat purple→magenta SaaS hero gradients** and **no gradient text** (`background-clip: text`). Our ember orange is a controlled accent on deep-space surfaces, not a loud gradient wash; the distinction is the whole point. No glassmorphism, no "trusted by" logo soup. If it could be any of a thousand AI tool landing pages, it has failed.
- **Default unstyled Bear Blog.** This template forks Bear Blog's default CSS. It must not still read as that raw, flat, placeholder-ish starter. Every section should look chosen, not inherited.
- **Decoration for its own sake.** No gratuitous motion, no font zoo, no visual noise competing with the content.

## Design Principles

1. **Practice what you preach.** A CMS that helps people build beautiful sites must itself look beautiful. The starter is the proof.
2. **Content is the hero.** Chrome (header, footer, nav) stays quiet and confident so the writing and imagery carry every page.
3. **Expert confidence, not loudness.** Restraint reads as craft. The ember accent and celestial motifs stay rationed (roughly a tenth of any screen); the interface never shouts over the content.
4. **Polished on first load.** No section may look unfinished, placeholder, or inherited. First impression is the whole job.
5. **Accessible by default.** Legibility and inclusion are design features here, not afterthoughts, and they reinforce the "crafted" personality.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target **WCAG 2.1 AA**: AA contrast on every text and interactive element in **both light and dark themes**, visible and well-styled focus states (the shared focus ring), semantic landmark markup, and respect for `prefers-reduced-motion` on block entrance animations. Legible, screen-native typography is a feature here, not an afterthought; accessibility should read as an intentional part of the craft rather than a compliance checkbox.
